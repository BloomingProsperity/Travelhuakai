# Shanghai Deep Research for Foreign Tourists

Research date: 2026-05-10  
Audience: first-time and repeat foreign tourists building an itinerary page for Shanghai  
Source posture: official-first, 2023+ sources only for travel facts and practical warnings; no OTA promo pages or generic listicles used as primary sources.

## Executive Positioning

Shanghai is not a single-center heritage city. For foreign visitors it works best as a layered city: treaty-port waterfront and old-city garden in Huangpu, former concession streets in Xuhui and Jing'an, vertical finance and theme-park tourism in Pudong, and water-town/suburban nature day trips in Qingpu, Songjiang, Minhang, Jiading, and Chongming.

For a first trip, the highest-value axis is:

- Day 1: The Bund, Nanjing East Road, Yu Garden, People's Square or Shanghai Museum.
- Day 2: Former French Concession/Wukang Road, Xintiandi, Jing'an Temple, food and cafes.
- Day 3: Lujiazui skyline, Shanghai Tower or Oriental Pearl, Museum of Art Pudong or West Bund, evening river views.
- Extra day: Zhujiajiao or Qibao for water-town texture; Disneyland if theme parks are a major priority; Suzhou or Hangzhou by high-speed rail if the visitor wants classical gardens or West Lake.

## 2. Top 15 Attractions for Foreign Tourists

### The Bund (外滩)
- District: Huangpu (黄浦区)
- Tier: must-visit
- Why visit: The Bund is Shanghai's essential postcard view: a 1.5-kilometer Huangpu River promenade where early-20th-century banks and trading houses face the Pudong skyline. It is the simplest place for a foreign visitor to understand Shanghai's treaty-port past and its present-day vertical finance identity in one frame.
- Booking: Walk-in; no passport or app needed for the promenade. River cruises, rooftop bars, and some museums/buildings require separate bookings.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: No normal public daily limit for the promenade, but crowd-control barriers and one-way flows are common on holidays, National Day, New Year, and major light-show nights. Sunrise is best for low crowds; evening is best for skyline lights.
- Hours: Outdoor promenade is effectively 24/7; individual buildings, Bund History Museum, shops, restaurants, and cruises vary. Closed-day pattern: none for the promenade.
- Foreign-card tickets: yes for no-ticket promenade; partial for cruises, bars, and restaurants. Use Alipay/WeChat Pay with a foreign card or major hotel/restaurant card terminals.
- English signage: yes/limited. Major street/metro signs are bilingual; deeper building history signage varies.
- Official URL: https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-ScenicSpots/20231205/584672cc6d044eabb5f7f6fc9049a19f.html

### Yu Garden (豫园)
- District: Huangpu (黄浦区)
- Tier: must-visit
- Why visit: Yu Garden is Shanghai's best central classical Chinese garden: Ming-dynasty rockeries, pavilions, ponds, zigzag bridges, old-city bazaar streets, and the City God Temple area compressed into a walkable historic quarter. For foreign tourists it is the easiest contrast to the Bund's European riverfront and Pudong's towers.
- Booking: Passport-friendly but China-mobile-app heavy. Day tickets are sold through the "Shanghai Yuyuan" / "yu-garden" official WeChat channel and at the South Gate ticket office; timed-entry batches may apply. Bring the passport or ID used for a discounted/free ticket.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: Entry is organized by reservation/time slots for special programs; lantern festival periods, weekends, public holidays, and summer evenings can be shoulder-to-shoulder.
- Hours: Standard garden hours are usually daytime, commonly 9:00-16:30 with last entry around 16:00; night-tour programs run seasonally, with official 2025 guidance listing May-October evening sessions on Tue/Wed/Fri/Sat/Sun and winter/spring sessions Fri-Sun. Closed-day pattern: check current official notice; many day schedules close Monday except holidays, while night-tour patterns differ.
- Foreign-card tickets: partial. Official WeChat/Alipay payments can work with linked foreign cards; ticket-office card support is less predictable, so carry a small cash/mobile-payment backup.
- English signage: limited/yes. Major names and directional signs are manageable; an English guide or audio guide improves the visit.
- Official URL: https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-ScenicSpots/20231205/dc76893b94c248d195eaf7f4d44c6597.html

### Shanghai Tower (上海中心大厦)
- District: Pudong New Area (浦东新区)
- Tier: must-visit
- Why visit: Shanghai Tower is the city's highest-observation-deck experience and the clearest "future Shanghai" attraction. The 118th-floor Top of Shanghai Observatory gives a 360-degree view over Lujiazui, the Bund, Suzhou Creek, and the city's scale, but it is also the attraction most affected by haze, rain, and low cloud.
- Booking: Passport-friendly real-name ticketing. Book via official Shanghai Tower channels, official WeChat, or on-site ticketing; keep passport details consistent with the booking.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: Timed/capacity controls apply during peak periods. Sunset slots and holidays sell out first. Do not book far ahead if the forecast shows fog, rain, or low visibility.
- Hours: Official city tourism page describes the site but not daily ticket hours; the observatory commonly operates daytime into evening, with current third-party schedule checks around 8:30/9:00 to 21:00/22:00. Closed-day pattern: usually daily, with weather or event closures possible. Verify same-week on official channels.
- Foreign-card tickets: partial. Major tourist venues should support overseas bank-card options, but the smoothest route is Alipay/WeChat Pay linked to a Visa/Mastercard or an on-site counter.
- English signage: yes. Observatory wayfinding and core visitor content are foreigner-friendly.
- Official URL: https://www.shanghaitower.com/
- Image (public-domain/CC): https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e0/Shanghai_-_Shanghai_Tower_-_0001.jpg
- Image credit: Stefan Fussan, CC BY-SA 3.0, 2016

### Lujiazui Skyline and Oriental Pearl Tower (陆家嘴 / 东方明珠)
- District: Pudong New Area (浦东新区)
- Tier: must-visit
- Why visit: Lujiazui is the compact skyline cluster opposite the Bund: Oriental Pearl Tower, Shanghai Tower, Shanghai World Financial Center, Jin Mao Tower, IFC, Super Brand Mall, riverside promenades, and hotel dining. Foreign tourists visit for iconic photos, sky decks, malls, and easy riverfront contrast with the Bund.
- Booking: Walk-in for the outdoor district and malls; ticketed sky decks use official ticketing, WeChat/app platforms, and on-site counters. Passport may be required for real-name tickets.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: Outdoor district has no normal daily limit, but towers and riverfront spots crowd heavily at sunset, Golden Week, May Day, New Year, and clear-weather weekends.
- Hours: Outdoor plazas and riverside areas are broadly open; malls and restaurants usually run late morning to evening; Oriental Pearl and other sky decks publish their own daily hours, commonly daytime into evening. Closed-day pattern: outdoor district none; tower operations vary.
- Foreign-card tickets: partial. Large malls/hotels usually accept international cards; tower ticketing may require mobile payment or a specific counter/terminal.
- English signage: yes in malls, metro, and major tower attractions; limited in some local restaurants.
- Official URL: https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-ScenicSpots/20231205/19a5f5184eca45728fd57a4d4c8efc61.html

### Xintiandi (新天地)
- District: Huangpu (黄浦区)
- Tier: worth-it
- Why visit: Xintiandi is Shanghai's most polished shikumen-renewal district: preserved stone-gate lane-house exteriors converted into restaurants, bars, boutiques, event spaces, and the nearby First CPC National Congress memorial area. Foreign visitors use it as an easy dining and nightlife base, not as an untouched old neighborhood.
- Booking: Walk-in for the block; restaurant bookings via phone, Dianping, hotel concierge, or official venue channels. Passport not needed unless visiting specific museums/exhibitions.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: No daily limit for the open block. Dinner, weekend, and event-night crowds are high; prices are premium by Shanghai standards.
- Hours: Outdoor block is open; restaurants, cafes, shops, and museums set their own hours. Closed-day pattern: none for the block; individual venues vary.
- Foreign-card tickets: yes/partial. Many higher-end restaurants and hotels accept foreign cards; smaller venues may prefer QR payments.
- English signage: yes. One of the easier districts for English menus/signage.
- Official URL: https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-HeritageZones/20240104/292e9fb43eec407783af83e547e61281.html

### Wukang Road and Wukang Mansion (武康路 / 武康大楼)
- District: Xuhui (徐汇区)
- Tier: must-visit
- Why visit: Wukang Road is the most visitor-friendly slice of the former French Concession/Hengfu historic area: plane-tree streets, garden villas, writer residences, cafes, Ferguson Lane, and the flatiron Wukang Mansion photo spot. It is better experienced as a slow walk than a single "attraction."
- Booking: Walk-in; no booking or passport needed for streets and exterior viewing. Private heritage tours or entry to museums/cafes require separate arrangements.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: No formal public daily limit, but Wukang Mansion's intersection is heavily controlled by police/barriers on weekends and holidays because of photo crowds.
- Hours: Streets are public all day; Wukang Road Tourist Information Centre and individual heritage venues/cafes have set hours. Closed-day pattern: none for the street.
- Foreign-card tickets: yes for no-ticket street; partial in cafes and restaurants.
- English signage: limited/yes. Street signs are bilingual; deeper heritage explanations are mixed.
- Official URL: https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-ScenicSpots/20231218/596192f5f59048bbbc3fa54d92304e93.html

### Jing'an Temple (静安寺)
- District: Jing'an (静安区)
- Tier: worth-it
- Why visit: Jing'an Temple is the most dramatic central temple contrast in Shanghai: golden roofs, incense, Buddhist halls, and ritual activity surrounded by luxury malls and elevated roads. It gives first-timers a religious/cultural stop without leaving the city center.
- Booking: Walk-in or official temple/WeChat ticketing depending on day and festival controls. Bring passport/cash/mobile payment backup; some travel-community reports note advance reservation through the official WeChat account during peak periods.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: No stable public daily limit found. Very crowded on lunar new year, Buddhist festivals, weekends, and rainy days when visitors combine it with nearby malls.
- Hours: Common visitor hours are approximately 7:30-17:00; confirm on the official temple site/WeChat for festival days. Closed-day pattern: generally open daily.
- Foreign-card tickets: partial/no. Expect mobile payment or cash-like fallback rather than a guaranteed international card terminal.
- English signage: limited. The visual experience is easy; detailed religious context benefits from a guide.
- Official URL: https://www.shjas.org/

### Tianzifang (田子坊)
- District: Huangpu (黄浦区)
- Tier: worth-it
- Why visit: Tianzifang is a dense Taikang Road lane network of shikumen alleys, small shops, galleries, snack stalls, cafes, bars, and preserved industrial/workshop traces. It is touristy, but still useful for foreign visitors who want old-lane scale without complicated navigation.
- Booking: Walk-in; no passport or app needed for the lanes.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: No daily limit; lanes are narrow and become congested on weekends, school holidays, and rainy-day shopping periods.
- Hours: Official city page lists the area as open 24/7 with free admission; individual shops and bars vary, often late morning to evening/night. Closed-day pattern: none for the area.
- Foreign-card tickets: yes for no-ticket area; partial for shops/restaurants. Small vendors may be QR-payment only.
- English signage: limited/yes. Tourist-facing shops have some English; deeper signage is inconsistent.

### Shanghai Museum on People's Square (上海博物馆)
- District: Huangpu (黄浦区)
- Tier: must-visit
- Why visit: Shanghai Museum is the strongest single stop for ancient Chinese art in central Shanghai: bronzes, ceramics, jade, calligraphy, painting, seals, furniture, and rotating exhibitions. It is especially valuable for foreign tourists who want cultural depth beyond skyline and shopping.
- Booking: Passport-friendly. Since Sept. 1, 2025, individual visitors at People's Square no longer need reservations for normal periods and can enter with valid ID/passport; peak periods and popular special exhibitions may still require WeChat reservation. Shanghai Museum East has its own entry rules.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: Crowd-control measures apply if daily or instantaneous capacity is exceeded. School holidays, national holidays, and blockbuster exhibitions require earlier planning.
- Hours: People's Square site: 9:00-17:00, last entry 16:00; closed Mondays except public holidays. Shanghai Museum East: 10:00-18:00, last entry 17:00; closed Tuesdays except public holidays.
- Foreign-card tickets: yes for regular free admission; partial for paid special exhibitions, shop, audio guide, and cafes.
- English signage: yes. Core galleries have strong bilingual labels; Shanghai Museum East offers paid English guided tours for key galleries.
- Official URL: https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-MuseumsGalleries/20231218/01fdd3f62d0c4877bc289ec0b1de6afa.html

### Shanghai Disneyland (上海迪士尼乐园)
- District: Pudong New Area (浦东新区)
- Tier: worth-it
- Why visit: Shanghai Disneyland is a full-day theme-park destination with China-specific scale, Enchanted Storybook Castle, TRON, Zootopia, Disneytown, and resort hotels. For Disney fans it is a must; for non-theme-park travelers it competes with an entire Shanghai city day.
- Booking: Passport-friendly but real-name ticketing is strict. Buy dated tickets through the Shanghai Disney Resort official website or official app; enter the same passport/travel-document details used for purchase and bring the original physical passport to the park.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: Park capacity and dated-ticket inventory are controlled. Chinese school holidays, weekends, Zootopia peaks, Halloween/Christmas, and Golden Week sell out or produce long waits. Use the official app for live waits, maps, reservations, and Premier Access.
- Hours: Hours vary by date; official search snapshot showed 8:30-21:30, but visitors must check the park calendar for the exact day. Closed-day pattern: generally open daily, with operational changes possible.
- Foreign-card tickets: yes/partial. Official channels and in-park large payment points support international-card routes, but foreign SMS verification and app/card processing can fail; set up Alipay and WeChat Pay in advance.
- English signage: yes. The park, app, maps, and guest services are foreigner-friendly.
- Official URL: https://www.shanghaidisneyresort.com/en/experience/attraction/theme-park

### Power Station of Art (上海当代艺术博物馆)
- District: Huangpu (黄浦区)
- Tier: worth-it
- Why visit: The Power Station of Art is Shanghai's major state contemporary-art museum and home of the Shanghai Biennale, housed in a converted power station near the Huangpu River and former Expo zone. It is best for visitors who want contemporary China and large-scale exhibitions rather than classical collections.
- Booking: Walk-in for some exhibitions; ticketed shows use official channels/WeChat and sometimes third-party ticketing. Passport usually not a barrier, but real-name ticketing can apply.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: No stable public daily limit found; popular exhibitions and Biennale periods can require timed booking or sell out.
- Hours: Common PSA pattern is Tuesday-Sunday, around 11:00-19:00 with last entry about one hour before close; closed Mondays except holidays. Verify exhibition-specific hours.
- Foreign-card tickets: partial. Some exhibitions are free; paid exhibitions are smoother with Alipay/WeChat Pay linked to a foreign card.
- English signage: limited/yes. Major shows often have English titles and some English text, but not always full translation.
- Official URL: https://www.powerstationofart.com/

### Zhujiajiao Water Town (朱家角古镇)
- District: Qingpu (青浦区)
- Tier: worth-it
- Why visit: Zhujiajiao is Shanghai's most useful classic water-town day trip: canals, Fangsheng Bridge, old lanes, temples, snack streets, boat rides, and enough scale to feel different from central Shanghai. It is touristy but still the cleanest answer for "water town without leaving Shanghai municipality."
- Booking: Walk-in for the town; paid gardens, boat rides, and some museums have separate tickets, often through on-site counters or WeChat. Passport rarely blocks basic entry.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: No normal daily limit for the town streets; boats and paid sites have capacity. Weekends, holidays, and autumn/spring fair-weather days are very crowded.
- Hours: Old town streets are broadly accessible all day; paid attractions, boats, shops, and restaurants commonly operate daytime to early evening. Closed-day pattern: outdoor town none; individual sites vary.
- Foreign-card tickets: partial. Expect QR payment/cash backup for boats and small shops; larger restaurants may accept cards.

### Qibao Ancient Town (七宝古镇)
- District: Minhang (闵行区)
- Tier: worth-it
- Why visit: Qibao is the easiest "old-town snack lane" from central Shanghai: Line 9 access, canals, bridges, old teahouse atmosphere, tangtuan, street food, and dense shops. It is smaller and more commercial than Zhujiajiao but far quicker to visit.

- English signage: limited. Station signs are bilingual; old-town food signs are mostly Chinese.

### Shanghai Natural History Museum (上海自然博物馆)
- District: Jing'an (静安区)
- Tier: worth-it
- Why visit: The Natural History Museum is the best family/science museum in central Shanghai, set within Jing'an Sculpture Park with an architecturally distinctive "green shell" building, dinosaur displays, natural-history galleries, and 4D/theater programs. It is especially strong for families, rainy days, and repeat visitors.
- Booking: Passport-friendly English ticket link exists for overseas visitors; official booking also uses the Shanghai Natural History Museum/SSTM ticket systems and WeChat. Tickets and 4D programs can release several days in advance during peak sessions.
- Daily limit / peak crowd: Tickets sell out during school holidays, summer Saturday evening sessions, and Golden Week. Book early if traveling with children or wanting theater seats.
- Hours: Regular baseline is 9:00-17:00 with last entry around 16:00/16:30, but 2025-2026 notices include extended Saturday/holiday hours and schedule changes. Closed-day pattern has commonly been Monday except holidays, but check the official ticket page because 2026 notices changed some venue schedules.
- Foreign-card tickets: partial. Online ticketing is mobile-payment oriented; use the English ticket link and prepare Alipay/WeChat Pay with a foreign card.
- English signage: yes/limited. Major wayfinding and some exhibits are bilingual; program language varies.

## 3. Food Districts and Dining Strengths

### Xintiandi (新天地)
Best for: polished international dining, cocktail bars, business dinners, easy English menus, date-night venues, fashion/event dining.  
What to send tourists for: a low-friction first night, shikumen atmosphere without difficult navigation, and a reliable post-museum/post-Bund dinner.  
Weakness: expensive and sanitized; not the place for bargain local food.

### Tianzifang and Dapuqiao/Taikang Road (田子坊 / 打浦桥)
Best for: casual snacks, cafes, bars, souvenir food, smaller lane-house venues.  
What to send tourists for: narrow-lane texture, coffee/beer breaks, easy browsing, and quick snacks.  
Weakness: touristy; not ideal for serious regional cuisine.

### Bund plus Lujiazui Skyline Dining (外滩 / 陆家嘴)
Best for: fine dining, hotel buffets, rooftop bars, afternoon tea, premium river-view restaurants, international hotel kitchens.  
What to send tourists for: one splurge meal, celebratory dinner, or skyline cocktail.  
Weakness: views often drive price more than food quality; reservations and dress codes can matter.

### Former French Concession: Anfu Road, Wuyuan Road, Wukang Road, Hengshan-Fuxing (安福路 / 五原路 / 武康路 / 衡复)
Best for: cafes, bakeries, wine bars, brunch, fusion, French/European dining, boutique Chinese restaurants, ferment-of-the-moment trends, small-format lifestyle restaurants.  
What to send tourists for: Shanghai's most walkable "eat and wander" neighborhood.  
Weakness: queues at viral cafes, high weekend crowding, and price creep around Anfu/Wukang.

### Jing'an and West Nanjing Road (静安 / 南京西路)
Best for: high-end Chinese, international chains, malls, bakeries, cocktail bars, tasting menus, hotel brunches, and reliable foreign-card acceptance.  
What to send tourists for: meals near Jing'an Temple, Zhangyuan, Natural History Museum, and premium shopping.  
Weakness: less old-neighborhood romance than Xuhui/Huangpu.

### Qibao Cheap Eats (七宝古镇)
Best for: tangtuan, skewers, local pastries, old-street snacks, teahouse nostalgia, budget grazing.  
What to send tourists for: a short metro-accessible food walk with water-town visuals.  
Weakness: extremely crowded and commercial on weekends; payment at small vendors is mostly QR/mobile.

### Xiaolongbao and Benbang Notes
- Xiaolongbao: Yu Garden/Nanxiang-branded outlets are convenient but touristy; Nanxiang in Jiading is the deeper food-history trip; Qibao offers snack browsing rather than definitive dumplings.
- Benbang cuisine: look around Huangpu, Jing'an, Xuhui, and old hotel/heritage restaurant zones for braised, sweet-savory Shanghai dishes such as hongshao rou, smoked fish, shrimp, river eel, and seasonal crab.
- International dining: strongest in Xintiandi, Jing'an, Xuhui/FFC, Changning/Gubei, and Lujiazui hotels.
- Hotel buffets: strongest around the Bund, Lujiazui, Jing'an, and West Nanjing Road.

## 4. Day-Trip-Worthy Areas

### Zhujiajiao Water Town (朱家角)
- Best for: classic Jiangnan canals without leaving Shanghai.
- Route: Metro Line 17 from Hongqiao Railway Station to Zhujiajiao Station, then taxi/bus/walk to the old town.
- Real timing: about 35-45 minutes on Line 17 from Hongqiao Railway Station to Zhujiajiao Station; about 75-100 minutes from People's Square depending on transfer and final walk/taxi.
- Station note: Hongqiao Railway Station is the Line 17 origin and is also a high-speed-rail/airport hub, so signs can be busy.
- Planning: go weekday morning or late afternoon; boat rides and paid gardens are extra.

### Qibao Ancient Town (七宝)
- Best for: fast snack-and-canal visit.
- Route: Metro Line 9 to Qibao Station.
- Real timing: about 15-20 minutes from Xujiahui; about 35-50 minutes from People's Square depending on transfer.
- Planning: good half-day or evening snack add-on; avoid weekend lunch peak.

### Sheshan and Songjiang (佘山 / 松江)
- Best for: hills, forest, Chenshan Botanical Garden, Guangfulin, Happy Valley, resort hotels.
- Route: Metro Line 9 toward Sheshan/Dongjing/Songjiang University Town, then taxi/shuttle depending on destination.
- Real timing: about 45-70 minutes from central Shanghai by metro plus last-mile transfer.
- Planning: combine Chenshan + Guangfulin or Sheshan + resort/Happy Valley; do not overpack because last-mile transfers take time.

### Chongming Island (崇明岛)
- Best for: wetlands, cycling, forest, birding, slow rural Shanghai.
- Route: no metro to main island. Use car/taxi/bus via Shanghai Yangtze River Tunnel/Bridge, or ferry routes depending on start point.
- Real timing: normally 1.5-2.5 hours from downtown each way.
- Planning: best as a full day or overnight; not a last-minute add-on after central sightseeing.

### Hangzhou and West Lake (杭州 / 西湖)
- Best for: one of China's most famous lake landscapes, temples, tea hills, and Song-dynasty capital atmosphere.
- Route: high-speed rail from Shanghai Hongqiao or Shanghai South/Shanghai Station to Hangzhou East or Hangzhou Station, then metro/taxi to West Lake.
- Real timing: fastest high-speed trains are about 45-60 minutes from Shanghai Hongqiao to Hangzhou East; add 30-45 minutes to reach West Lake depending on station and transport.
- Booking: use official 12306 English site/app or a hotel/travel desk; passport is required for China rail real-name ticketing and station entry.

### Suzhou Gardens (苏州)
- Best for: UNESCO classical gardens, canals, silk, slower old-city texture.
- Route: high-speed rail from Shanghai Hongqiao or Shanghai Station to Suzhou/Suzhou North, then Suzhou Metro/taxi to Humble Administrator's Garden, Lingering Garden, Pingjiang Road, or Tiger Hill.
- Real timing: fastest high-speed trains are about 25-35 minutes station-to-station; add 20-40 minutes for city transfer.
- Booking: use official 12306 English site/app; arrive early at Hongqiao because passport gates, security, and walking distances take time.

## 5. Practical Pitfalls Specific to Shanghai

### Hongqiao Name Confusion
Hongqiao is not one building. Foreign tourists commonly mix up:

- Shanghai Hongqiao International Airport (SHA), with T1 and T2 on different sides of the airfield.
- Shanghai Hongqiao Railway Station (上海虹桥站), the huge high-speed rail station.
- Hongqiao long-distance bus/transport hub functions around the same integrated area.

T2 and the railway station are connected but still require walking time; T1 is separate and usually needs Line 10/shuttle/taxi. Build in 45-60 minutes if changing between air and high-speed rail with luggage.

### Maglev Goes Only PVG to Longyang Road
The Shanghai Maglev is fast and fun but not a city-center train. It links Pudong International Airport (PVG) and Longyang Road, where visitors transfer to metro/taxi. It does not go to the Bund, People's Square, Hongqiao, or Lujiazui. Official notices list Longyang Road first train around 6:45 and last train around 21:40; late arrivals often need Metro Line 2, airport bus, or taxi instead.

### Tea House Scam: Nanjing Road, People's Park, Bund Edge
The classic setup remains current: friendly English-speaking strangers invite tourists to practice English, see art, drink tea, or visit a "traditional" venue, then leave them with an inflated bill. The highest-risk zone is Nanjing East Road/People's Square/People's Park/Bund tourist corridors. Rule: do not follow newly met strangers to tea, bars, galleries, massage venues, or "student art" shops.

### Last Metro Times Are Earlier Than Many Visitors Expect
Shanghai Metro is excellent but not a 24-hour system. Many lines/stations have last trains around 22:30-23:00, with direction-specific exceptions. Suburban returns from Disney, Zhujiajiao, Sheshan, Hongqiao, or PVG need last-train checking. Always check station-specific last trains in the Shanghai Metro app/Daduhui app or station timetable, not just "Line 2 closes at X."

### Shanghai Disney Is Not Hong Kong Disney
Shanghai Disneyland and Hong Kong Disneyland have different official apps, accounts, ticketing systems, passport/ID requirements, calendars, Premier Access products, and transport. A Hong Kong Disney ticket/account does not solve Shanghai entry. Shanghai uses real-name ticketing; bring the physical passport used for purchase.

### Shanghai Tower Visibility Is Weather-Dependent
The observation deck can be poor value in haze, rain, low cloud, or typhoon-season weather. If possible, book close to the visit date and choose morning after rain or a clear late afternoon. If clouds are covering the upper Lujiazui towers from the Bund, do not expect a good deck view.
