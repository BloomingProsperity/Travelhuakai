## SECTION A - Personal Travel Blogs / Forum Threads / Practical-Pitfall Sources

### PILLAR 1: VISA + ENTRY

**A-01 - Third-country rule silently rejects same-country round-trips**
The 240-hour visa-free transit requires your onward ticket to leave China for a DIFFERENT country than your origin. New York to Shanghai to New York is denied at immigration. New York to Shanghai to Singapore is approved. Hong Kong, Macau, and Taiwan count as separate regions for this rule.
Source: https://chinafortravelers.com/guides/china-visa-free-transit/
Author: ChinaForTravelers editors | Date: 2025 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-02 - 240-hour clock starts at immigration clearance, not wheels-down**
The 10-day countdown begins when the officer stamps your passport, not when you land. If you land at 23:00 and clear immigration at 00:30, you lose nearly a full day. Many travelers miscalculate and overstay.
Source: https://chinafortravelers.com/guides/china-visa-free-transit/
Author: ChinaForTravelers editors | Date: 2025 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-03 - Digital Arrival Card mandatory since November 2025**
The NIA digital arrival card (s.nia.gov.cn) must be filled within 72 hours before arrival. Missing it causes a secondary desk stop at immigration adding 20-40 minutes. Not mentioned in airline boarding instructions.
Source: https://www.smartshanghai.com/articles/shanghai-life/the-faq-china-visa-on-arrivals-need-to-know
Author: SmartShanghai editorial | Date: 2025 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-04 - Wrong immigration queue adds 30 minutes**
Visa-free transit travelers must use the dedicated TWOV counter, not the general immigration line. Pudong Terminal 2 marks it clearly; Beijing T3 barely labels it.
Source: https://chinafortravelers.com/guides/china-visa-free-transit/
Author: ChinaForTravelers editors | Date: 2025 | City scope: BJ/SH

**A-05 - Extension requests almost always fail; plan before arrival**
SmartShanghai staff attempted an extension and it was rejected for "insufficient reason." Processing takes up to 7 working days, so you must apply at least 8 days before expiry.
Source: https://www.smartshanghai.com/articles/shanghai-life/the-faq-china-visa-on-arrivals-need-to-know
Author: SmartShanghai editorial | Date: 2025 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-06 - Overstay penalty is entry ban, not just a fine**
Overstays beyond a few days trigger a 1-5 year re-entry ban in addition to fines starting at 500 CNY/day.
Source: https://chinafortravelers.com/guides/china-visa-free-transit/
Author: ChinaForTravelers editors | Date: 2025 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-07 - Unlicensed guesthouses cannot legally host foreign guests**
Chinese law requires accommodation to register foreign guests with police. Budget guesthouses and Airbnb-style listings may lack the required license. Both tourist and host face fines.
Source: https://www.thechinajourney.com/china-travel-tips/
Author: TheChina Journey editorial | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: All-of-China


### PILLAR 2: PAYMENTS

**A-08 - Alipay TourCard does not support American Express**
AmEx is excluded from the Alipay TourCard / international version as of late 2025. Workaround: use a Visa or Mastercard credit card (not debit).
Source: https://wildchina.com/2025/10/guide-to-using-alipay-2025/
Author: WildChina editorial | Date: October 2025 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-09 - Debit cards fail more often than credit cards on Alipay/WeChat**
Visa and Mastercard debit cards often pass the linkage step but silently fail at checkout. The same number as a credit card succeeds. Bring a credit card, not just a debit card.
Source: https://rachelmeetschina.com/2024/10/29/traveling-to-china-how-to-connect-your-foreign-bank-card-to-the-alipay-and-wechat/
Author: Rachel Meets China | Date: October 2024 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-10 - WeChat account can be temporarily restricted by automated security flags**
A traveller's WeChat account was restricted in November 2024 after triggering an automated security check (possibly a login from a new device abroad). Restoration takes 24-72 hours. Set up both Alipay AND WeChat before departure.
Source: https://www.marketplace.org/story/2024/09/26/e-payments-wechat-are-essential-in-china-but-still-tricky-for-foreign-visitors
Author: Marketplace/NPR | Date: September 2024 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-11 - Alipay TourCard charges a 5% conversion fee on every top-up**
On top of whatever foreign transaction fee your home bank charges. For a 5,000 CNY top-up, 250 CNY is lost immediately. Travellers with WeChat Pay linked directly to a card can avoid this fee for WeChat transactions.
Source: https://wildchina.com/2025/10/guide-to-using-alipay-2025/
Author: WildChina editorial | Date: October 2025 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-12 - Many small vendors have no card reader and no change for large notes**
Cash is legally required to be accepted but in practice many street food stalls have no card reader and no change for 100 CNY notes. Carry 10-50 CNY notes as backup.
Source: https://www.smartshanghai.com/articles/essentials/how-to-pay-in-china-as-a-foreign-tourist
Author: SmartShanghai editorial | Date: 2024 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-13 - Revolut and virtual banks work more reliably than traditional banks on Alipay**
Revolut, Wise, and similar fintech-issued Mastercards succeed at a higher rate than traditional high-street bank cards. Some traditional banks block overseas digital-wallet binding by default.
Source: https://rachelmeetschina.com/2024/10/29/traveling-to-china-how-to-connect-your-foreign-bank-card-to-the-alipay-and-wechat/
Author: Rachel Meets China | Date: October 2024 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-14 - WeChat QR code fraud: scammers replace merchant codes at market stalls**
In Guangzhou's wholesale market district (Yide Road, Zhanxi Road) and Shanghai street food areas, scammers replace legitimate vendor QR codes with their own. Always verify the merchant name shown in the app after scanning before confirming payment.
Source: https://beforeyougotravels.com/destinations/guangzhou
Author: Before You Go Travels | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: GZ/SH


### PILLAR 3: TRANSPORT

**A-15 - Beijing contactless Visa/Mastercard tap works since Sept 2024 but only at specific gate lanes**
Since September 13, 2024, all 490 Beijing subway stations accept contactless Visa/Mastercard tap-and-go. However, designated international-card turnstiles are at specific lane positions (typically at the end of a row). Tapping a regular domestic-only gate still fails.
Source: https://english.beijing.gov.cn/livinginbeijing/transportation/beijingsubway/202409/t20240914_3891497.html
Author: Beijing Municipal Government | Date: September 2024 | City scope: BJ

**A-16 - Alipay transit QR code must be activated separately for each city**
The transport QR code in Alipay is city-specific. Activating for Beijing does not activate for Shanghai, Guangzhou, or Shenzhen. Each requires separate in-app setup under the "Transport" tab, selecting the target city before arriving at the turnstile.
Source: https://chinaguidelines.com/en/posts/public-transport
Author: China Guidelines editorial | Date: 2026 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-17 - Alipay transit QR code expires in 5 minutes; regenerate before reaching the turnstile**
The code times out. If you generated it while walking and 5+ minutes passed, the turnstile rejects it. Generate fresh immediately before scanning.
Source: https://chinaguidelines.com/en/posts/public-transport
Author: China Guidelines editorial | Date: 2026 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-18 - 12306 passport identity verification takes 3-7 days; plan ahead**
The online passport verification at 12306.cn requires a passport photo and a selfie holding the passport. Processing takes 3-5 days but can reach 7 during peak periods. Last-minute train bookings with a new 12306 account are effectively impossible. Workaround: use Trip.com, which verifies faster (1-2 hours in most cases).
Source: https://chinaguidelines.com/en/posts/high-speed-train
Author: China Guidelines editorial | Date: 2026 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-19 - Self-service ticket machines at stations do not accept passports; use staffed windows**
Automatic ticket collection machines process Chinese ID cards only. Foreign passport holders must queue at staffed ticket windows. At large stations (Beijing West, Shanghai Hongqiao) this queue can be 20-30 minutes on busy days.
Source: https://chinaguidelines.com/en/posts/high-speed-train
Author: China Guidelines editorial | Date: 2026 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-20 - Adding extra passengers to a 12306 booking requires separate 3-7 day verification per person**
Group travel through 12306 means each passenger needs their own passport verification cycle. For a family of 4, all 4 passports must clear before any tickets can be issued. Use Trip.com for groups to avoid this.
Source: https://chinaguidelines.com/en/posts/high-speed-train
Author: China Guidelines editorial | Date: 2026 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-21 - Beijing has 5+ major rail stations; selecting the wrong one is a costly mistake**
Beijing stations: Beijing (main), Beijing West, Beijing South, Beijing North, Beijing Chaoyang. Trains to different destinations depart from different stations 30-60 minutes apart by metro. Verify the specific station when booking, not just the city name.
Source: https://chinaguidelines.com/en/posts/high-speed-train
Author: China Guidelines editorial | Date: 2026 | City scope: BJ

**A-22 - Black taxi touts at Beijing Capital T3 approach tourists before the official queue**
Men posing as "helpers" near the T3 taxi information desk single out foreign-looking travellers and offer rides at 3-4x meter rates. Legitimate Beijing taxis display "Jing B" (capital B) license plates. Anyone who approaches you first is not legitimate.
Source: https://www.flyertalk.com/forum/china/1897228-beijing-airport-taxi-scam-attempt-taxi-information-desk.html
Author: FlyerTalk community reports | Date: ongoing | City scope: BJ

**A-23 - Black taxi touts at Guangzhou South Station charge 3-5x normal rates**
Unlicensed cab touts work the main exits of Guangzhou South (high-speed rail) and Guangzhou East stations. Use DiDi from the designated app pickup zone inside the station, or walk past all touts to the official metered taxi rank.
Source: https://beforeyougotravels.com/destinations/guangzhou
Author: Before You Go Travels | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: GZ

**A-24 - DiDi: use the domestic "Chuxing" app, not the international "DiDi Rider" app**
The international "DiDi Rider" app has limited coverage in mainland China. Download "DiDi Chuxing" (the mainland version), which has a full English interface and broader vehicle availability. Payment via Alipay or WeChat linked to a foreign card works reliably in the domestic version.
Source: https://wise.com/sg/blog/how-to-use-didi-china
Author: Wise editorial | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-25 - Standard Octopus card does NOT work on Shenzhen Metro**
Hong Kong tourists visiting Shenzhen often expect Octopus to work as it does in HK. The standard Octopus card is rejected at Shenzhen Metro turnstiles. Options: (1) Alipay QR code activated for Shenzhen, (2) Shenzhen Tong physical card (20 CNY deposit), (3) "Hu Tong Xing" dual-card purchased in Shenzhen (68 CNY) containing both an Octopus purse (HKD) and Shenzhen Tong purse (CNY) - but each must be topped up separately in the relevant city.
Source: https://www.eyeshenzhen.com/content/2024-04/01/content_30840321.htm
Author: EyeShenzhen / Shenzhen government | Date: April 2024 | City scope: SZ

**A-26 - Shanghai metro refunds only processed at service windows 11:00-14:00 daily**
If you buy a wrong ticket at a Shanghai metro vending machine, refunds are only processed at pink service counters during 11:00-14:00. Outside those hours there is no cash refund. Bring original ticket and passport.
Source: https://travelchina.tips/blogs/practical-guides/shanghai-metro-faq-for-tourists
Author: TravelChina.tips editorial | Date: 2025 | City scope: SH

**A-27 - Shanghai taxi reliability: stick to yellow (Qiangsheng) or white (Jinjiang) companies**
Other colors (red, dark blue, or other liveries) are smaller companies with fewer accountability mechanisms. DiDi is preferable, but if flagging a street taxi in Shanghai, stick to yellow or white.
Source: https://travelchinawith.me/china-travel-blog/taxi-and-didi-scams-in-china/
Author: Travel China With Me | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: SH


### PILLAR 4: FOOD

**A-28 - "Vegetarian" dishes are often cooked in pork-based stock or lard**
Ordering a vegetable dish at a regular restaurant does not guarantee it is vegetarian. Many dishes are blanched in pork bone broth or stir-fried in lard. Only Buddhist vegetarian restaurants (Su Shi, the 素食 sign) reliably avoid pork products, though those use seitan (pure gluten) mock meats - a separate hazard for coeliac diners.
Source: https://alittleadrift.com/vegetarian-food-in-china/
Author: A Little Adrift | Date: 2024 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-29 - Soy sauce contains wheat; "no soy sauce" request rarely eliminates gluten**
Soy sauce (jiangyou) contains wheat and is used in the wok for nearly every stir-fry, marinade, and sauce. Asking a waiter to remove it typically means the dish arrives with less, not none. Gluten-free eating requires ordering steamed dishes and specifying this in writing to kitchen staff.
Source: https://sinotales.com/interests/food-drink/first-time-travel-china-food-allergies/
Author: Sinotales editorial | Date: 2025 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-30 - Hidden shellfish in non-seafood dishes: dried shrimp and shrimp paste as invisible umami base**
Dried shrimp (xia pi) and shrimp paste (xia jiang) are routinely added to vegetable stir-fries, congee, and soups as flavour enhancers. Menu descriptions show no seafood. Show a written allergy card in Chinese directly to kitchen staff, not just the waiter.
Source: https://sinotales.com/interests/food-drink/first-time-travel-china-food-allergies/
Author: Sinotales editorial | Date: 2025 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-31 - Shared woks mean cross-contamination even when staff understand the allergy**
Traditional kitchens use one or two woks for all dishes, rinsed with hot water between orders. Even when the chef is aware of an allergy, shared wok residue transfers. Night market shared fryers compound this. Severe allergies require restaurants with visibly separated cooking equipment.
Source: https://travelchinawith.me/china-travel-blog/survival-guide-for-food-allergies-in-china/
Author: Travel China With Me | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-32 - Tea house scam: strangers invite you to "practice English" then present a 500-5000 CNY bill**
Documented at Wangfujing and Houhai (BJ), Nanjing Road and People's Park (SH), Shamian Island (GZ). The acquaintance disappears after ordering; staff block exits until payment or you call police. If you enter a venue with strangers, demand to see a menu with prices before sitting.
Source: https://www.china-mike.com/china-travel-tips/avoiding-scams/
Author: China Mike | Date: updated 2024-2025 | City scope: BJ/SH/GZ

**A-33 - Short-changing at tourist-area restaurants: bills rounded up by 20-100 CNY**
Documented near Shamian Island (GZ), Yu Garden area (SH), Wangfujing (BJ). Count your change immediately. Insist on an itemised receipt (fapiao) before paying.
Source: https://beforeyougotravels.com/destinations/guangzhou
Author: Before You Go Travels | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: GZ/SH/BJ

**A-34 - Tap water is not potable anywhere in the 4 cities**
All four cities' tap water requires boiling before drinking. Hotel rooms provide a kettle or filtered dispenser. Ice in budget restaurants may be made from unfiltered tap water. Bottled water is widely available at 2-5 CNY for 550ml.
Source: https://www.thechinajourney.com/china-travel-tips/
Author: TheChina Journey editorial | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-35 - Guangzhou Canton Fair period (April/October) causes price spikes and fake restaurant bookings**
During the bi-annual Canton Fair, restaurant and hotel supply is overwhelmed by 200,000+ trade visitors. Prices near Pazhou and Tianhe jump 30-60%. Fake reservation platforms ask for deposits. Book via Dianping or Meituan only.
Source: https://beforeyougotravels.com/destinations/guangzhou
Author: Before You Go Travels | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: GZ


### PILLAR 5: EMERGENCY / MEDICAL

**A-36 - Ambulances are slow; taxis reach hospitals faster in most non-spinal emergencies**
Emergency number is 120. But ambulances in Chinese cities are not equipped to Western paramedic standards and travel times in urban traffic exceed 20 minutes. For non-spinal emergencies, a taxi to the nearest 3A hospital is consistently faster and recommended by long-term expats.
Source: https://www.thechinaguide.com/blog/healthcare-and-insurance
Author: The China Guide editorial | Date: 2024 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-37 - Public hospitals require upfront cash deposit before admission; foreign insurance rarely direct-bills**
Hospitalization requires a 5,000-20,000 CNY deposit paid before treatment beyond emergency stabilisation. Ambulance fees (~7 CNY/km, 80 CNY/hour waiting) also billed upfront. Most travel insurance policies reimburse after the fact. Verify your insurer's direct-billing hospital list before travelling.
Source: https://www.expatden.com/china/hospital-in-shanghai/
Author: ExpatDen editorial | Date: 2024 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-38 - Public hospital registration requires a medical card (Yiliao Ka) before any appointment**
New patients must obtain a medical card first: passport and 50 CNY at the registration desk, 15-20 minutes. Arriving expecting a direct walk-in consultation is a common mistake.
Source: https://www.expatden.com/china/hospital-in-shanghai/
Author: ExpatDen editorial | Date: 2024 | City scope: SH/BJ

**A-39 - International hospital departments cost 6-12x standard Chinese rates**
International-standard private hospitals (Beijing United Family, PUMCH international wing, Raffles Hospital Shanghai) offer English-speaking staff, but outpatient registration alone costs 300-600 CNY vs 10-30 CNY at standard public departments. Without international health insurance with direct billing, a single appointment can cost 2,000-5,000+ CNY.
Source: https://www.internationalinsurance.com/countries/china/hospitals/
Author: International Insurance editorial | Date: 2024 | City scope: BJ/SH

**A-40 - Western prescription medications unavailable; bring your own supply plus 20% extra**
Chinese pharmacies do not stock most Western brand-name medications. Controlled substances (certain pain medications, ADHD drugs, anxiety medications) are severely restricted and may be confiscated at customs without a physician letter. Bring a full supply in original labelled bottles.
Source: https://www.thechinajourney.com/china-travel-tips/
Author: TheChina Journey editorial | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: All-of-China

**A-41 - Beijing has 3 emergency numbers; 999 (private ambulance) is sometimes faster than 120**
Beijing emergency: 120 (public emergency medical), 999 (Red Cross/private ambulance, faster in some districts), 110 (police). US Embassy medical assistance: +86-10-8531-4000. Beijing United Family 24h: +86-10-5927-7120. PUMCH 24h emergency: +86-10-69155288.
Source: https://english.beijing.gov.cn/quickguideservices/medicalguide/index.html
Author: Beijing Municipal Government | Date: 2024 | City scope: BJ

**A-42 - Foreign guest police registration required within 24 hours even at private accommodation**
Hotels do this automatically. At an Airbnb or private rental, you or your host must register at the local police station within 24 hours. Failure is a fineable offence for both parties.
Source: https://www.thechinajourney.com/china-travel-tips/
Author: TheChina Journey editorial | Date: 2025-2026 | City scope: All-of-China
